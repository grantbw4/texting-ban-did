1. Introduction

Distracted driving has emerged as a major public health concern in the United States. According to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, distracted driving claimed 3,522 lives in 2021 alone. In response, many states have enacted laws prohibiting the use of handheld electronic devices while driving.

However, not all handheld device bans are created equal. A critical distinction lies in the enforcement mechanism: primary enforcement laws allow police officers to stop and cite drivers solely for device use, while secondary enforcement laws only permit citations after a stop for another violation. This distinction may significantly affect the deterrent effect of these policies.

This study estimates the causal effect of adopting primary enforcement handheld device bans on traffic fatalities using modern staggered difference-in-differences methods. The staggered adoption of these laws across states and time creates a natural experiment, but standard econometric approaches can produce biased estimates in this setting.

4. Empirical Strategy

4.1 The Problem with Traditional TWFE

The standard approach to difference-in-differences with staggered adoption is two-way fixed effects (TWFE):

Yit = αi + λt + β · Treatedit + εit

However, Goodman-Bacon (2021) shows that the TWFE estimator is a weighted average of all possible 2×2 DiD comparisons, including "forbidden" comparisons that use already-treated units as controls. When treatment effects are heterogeneous over time, this can lead to biased estimates.

4.3 Callaway & Sant'Anna Estimator

To address these concerns, I implement the Callaway & Sant'Anna (2021) estimator, which:

  • Only uses valid comparisons (never-treated or not-yet-treated as controls)
  • Estimates separate ATT(g,t) for each cohort g at each time t
  • Provides flexible aggregation schemes for summary measures
